About

PhyloTree is an interactive viewer for the Tree of Life - the family tree that shows the evolutionary relationships between all living organisms. Figuring out the tree of life is an ongoing research project that that thousands of labs across the world are working on. We don't yet know how all species are related to each other, but we already have a pretty good idea about many relationships.

Since there isn't a single integrated picture of Tree of Life yet, PhyloTree is based on a very rough approximation of the Tree of Life - the taxonomic categorization of species. Using taxonomy as a stand-in for evolutionary relationships is quite limiting in many respects, but it does provide an opportunity to address some of the challenges that arise when visualizing hundreds of thousands of species. It also starts to give a sense of how we might interact with the visualizations of the Tree of Life as it comes into sharper focus.

For this project, we decided to focus on exploring the history of the description of species. Species descriptions are scientific manuscripts that name species, describe their anatomy and other aspects of their biology, and clearly indicate how they differ from other known species. We can explore the history of scientific discovery by visualizing how newly discovered species were added to the Tree of Life since the first formal species descriptions by Carl Lineus in the 18th century. The broad diversity of some groups of species was already roughed in very early on (as for Mamallia), with a small number of species added here and there as the years went on. Other groups were discovered more recently and then descibed in explosive bursts, and others (like Cnidaria) fall somewhere in between.

Using PhyloTree

There are three ways to interact with the Tree of Life through the PhyloTree viewer.

The Tree

Each taxonomic unit, whether it is a species or a group of species, is represented as an animated, interactive node. Since the tree of life is far too large to fit into a single web browser window, only a subset of nodes for a particualr group are usually shown at a time. The degree of data reduction depends on the scale the tree is viewed at.

The ancestors of all the nodes in the tree are shown in orange. These include the root (the most recent common ancestor), as well as the ancestors of the root, which are shown along the top of the viewer.

When you click on a node, it is highlighted. It turns yellow, and information about the node is shown in the "Highlighted Node" section. Click "Reroot here" to change the root of the tree that is shown. If you reroot on one of the blue nodes in the tree, you are zooming in on the Tree of life. If you click on an orange node at the top of the page, you are zooming out.

The year of species description

Move the "Discovery Date" slider to see the history of species description. Only the species that were described prior to the date indicated by the slider are shown. Nodes become red as they are about to appear or disappear.

Organism Name

Use these text boxes to search for particular species or groups of species. Refer to ITIS for more information on these names, or to look up taxonomic serial numbers if you prefer to browse that way.

Implementation

The source code for PhyloTree is available at github. This code builds on phylogeny-d3, an earlier project by Casey Dunn.
